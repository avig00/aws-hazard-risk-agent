NOAA Atlantic Hurricane Season Summary and County Risk Assessment — Gulf Coast and Atlantic Seaboard

HURRICANE CLIMATOLOGY IN THE UNITED STATES

Atlantic hurricanes are among the costliest natural disasters affecting the United States.
Since 1980, hurricanes and tropical storms have caused over $1.1 trillion in inflation-
adjusted losses and more than 6,700 direct deaths. The Atlantic hurricane season officially
runs June 1 through November 30, though storms have occurred outside this window.

NOAA classifies Atlantic hurricanes on the Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Wind Scale:
- Category 1: 74–95 mph sustained winds
- Category 2: 96–110 mph sustained winds
- Category 3 (Major): 111–129 mph sustained winds
- Category 4 (Major): 130–156 mph sustained winds
- Category 5 (Major): 157+ mph sustained winds

PRIMARY DAMAGE MECHANISMS

Storm Surge (50–60% of hurricane fatalities):
Storm surge is the abnormal rise of water generated by a storm's winds and pressure.
Surge height depends on storm intensity, size, forward speed, angle of approach, and
local bathymetry. A slow-moving Category 4 can produce 15–20 feet of surge in shallow
Gulf Coast bays — enough to inundate second-floor homes.

High Winds (primary structure damage mechanism):
Wind damage follows a cubic relationship with wind speed — doubling wind speed roughly
octuples the destructive force. Category 3+ hurricanes can demolish non-hardened structures
and routinely remove roofs even from well-constructed homes.

Inland Flooding / Freshwater Flooding:
Slow-moving storms can dump extreme rainfall — Hurricane Harvey (2017) produced 60.58
inches of rain over Harris County, TX — the highest rainfall total ever recorded in the
continental United States from a single storm.

HIGH-RISK COUNTIES: GULF COAST

Harris County, Texas (FIPS: 48201):
Harris County (Houston metro) is one of the most hurricane-vulnerable counties due to its
position on Galveston Bay, flat terrain, and extensive bayou drainage system.

Hurricane history (direct landfalls/major impacts since 2000):
- Hurricane Ike (2008): Cat 2 landfall on Galveston Island, 20-foot storm surge,
  $30 billion damage; largest FEMA individual assistance registration for any hurricane
- Hurricane Harvey (2017): Catastrophic Category 4 landfall near Rockport; looped over
  Houston for 4 days, $125 billion total damage; 36,000+ rescues in Harris County

NRI Hurricane EAL: $1.2 billion/year

Monroe County, Florida / Florida Keys (FIPS: 12087):
The Florida Keys face the highest hurricane wind exposure in the continental U.S. due to
their southern latitude, peninsular exposure, and low elevation (average 5 feet above MSL).

Notable events:
- Hurricane Irma (2017): Cat 4 landfall on Cudjoe Key; Lower Keys experienced Cat 5 winds
  offshore; $1.3 billion damage to Monroe County; 25% of homes destroyed or heavily damaged
- Labor Day Hurricane (1935): Cat 5, 892 mbar — still lowest barometric pressure measured
  at U.S. landfall; destroyed the Overseas Railroad, 400+ deaths

NRI Hurricane EAL for Monroe County: $280 million/year
NRI Hurricane Risk Percentile: 99th nationally

Galveston County, Texas (FIPS: 48167):
Galveston Island was the site of the deadliest U.S. natural disaster — the 1900 hurricane
that killed an estimated 6,000–12,000 people. Today, the Galveston seawall provides some
protection, but storm surge from major hurricane landfalls can overtop the 17-foot seawall.

HIGH-RISK COUNTIES: ATLANTIC SEABOARD

Miami-Dade County, Florida (FIPS: 12086):
- Highest hurricane EAL in the United States: $4.2 billion/year
- Direct landfalls within 50 miles since 1900: 11 major hurricanes
- Hurricane Andrew (1992): Cat 5 landfall in southern Miami-Dade; $27 billion damage
  (1992 dollars); destroyed 25,000 homes, damaged 100,000 more
- Most valuable coastal property exposure in the hurricane belt

Broward County, Florida (FIPS: 12011):
Broward (Fort Lauderdale) closely mirrors Miami-Dade in vulnerability. Hurricane Wilma
(2005) caused $20 billion statewide; Broward was among the hardest-hit Florida counties.

New Hanover County, North Carolina (FIPS: 37129):
Home to Wilmington, NC, at the confluence of the Cape Fear River and Atlantic Ocean.
- Hurricane Florence (2018): Cat 1 landfall at Wrightsville Beach, dumped record 35.9
  inches on Elizabethtown; $22 billion North Carolina damage; New Hanover experienced
  5–10 feet of storm surge

HURRICANE RISK TRENDS

The 2020 and 2021 hurricane seasons were among the most active on record:
- 2020: 30 named storms, 14 hurricanes, 7 major hurricanes
- 2021: 21 named storms including Hurricane Ida (Cat 4, $75B, 55 deaths in Louisiana
  and $14B additional deaths in northeastern U.S. from inland flooding)
- 2022: Hurricane Ian (Cat 4, $112 billion), second costliest U.S. hurricane after
  Katrina; Lee County, FL bore the worst damage with catastrophic storm surge

Intensification trends:
Research indicates Atlantic hurricanes are intensifying more rapidly (rapid intensification
defined as ≥35 mph increase in 24 hours) due to warmer ocean temperatures. Hurricane
Michael (2018) went from Cat 2 to Cat 5 in 24 hours before landfall.

SOCIAL VULNERABILITY AND HURRICANE RISK

The NRI's social vulnerability modifier amplifies hurricane risk scores in counties with:
- High poverty rates (>20%): Limited financial resources for evacuation and recovery
- High mobile home percentage: Manufactured housing provides minimal hurricane protection
- Elderly populations: Mobility limitations impede evacuation compliance
- Language isolation: Limited English proficiency reduces warning communication effectiveness

Cameron County, TX (FIPS: 48061) — Brownsville area — illustrates this interaction:
- High hurricane exposure (Gulf of Mexico border)
- 33% poverty rate, high mobile home density
- NRI Social Vulnerability Index: 88th percentile
- Net risk score elevated 40% above physical hazard alone due to vulnerability

NOAA HURRICANE PREPAREDNESS RESOURCES

1. Know your zone: Evacuate when ordered for your hurricane evacuation zone
2. Surge maps: Use NOAA SLOSH (Sea, Lake, Overland Surges from Hurricanes) model maps
   to understand flood risk at your address
3. Shelter: If not evacuating, shelter in a hardened interior room on the highest floor
   not subject to flooding; avoid coastal and bayou-adjacent areas
4. Insurance: Standard homeowner's insurance does not cover storm surge; NFIP flood
   insurance and supplemental wind coverage are essential for Gulf/Atlantic coastal residents
5. FEMA preparedness: 72-hour emergency kit minimum; register medical needs with county
   emergency management for special needs shelters
