NOAA National Severe Storms Laboratory — Tornado Risk in the Central United States

TORNADO ALLEY: DEFINITION AND GEOGRAPHY

"Tornado Alley" is a colloquial term for the region of the central United States that
experiences the highest frequency and intensity of tornadoes. The core of Tornado Alley
encompasses parts of Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, and South Dakota, though high
tornado frequency extends into Missouri, Iowa, Illinois, and Indiana — sometimes called
the "Expanded Tornado Alley" or "Dixie Alley" in the Southeast.

The geographic concentration of tornadoes in this region results from unique atmospheric
conditions: warm, moist air from the Gulf of Mexico collides with cool, dry air from the
Rocky Mountains and cold Arctic air masses from Canada. This collision produces the severe
thunderstorm environments that spawn tornadoes more frequently than anywhere else on Earth.

TORNADO CLIMATOLOGY

Annual tornado statistics (NOAA Storm Prediction Center, 2000–2023 average):
- Average U.S. tornadoes per year: 1,200–1,400
- Average U.S. tornado deaths per year: 70–80
- Average annual property damage: $5–8 billion

Peak tornado season:
- Southern Plains (TX, OK): March–May
- Northern Plains and Midwest: May–June
- Southeast (Dixie Alley): November–March (second peak)

Tornado intensity (Enhanced Fujita Scale):
- EF0–EF1: Weak (winds 65–110 mph) — ~80% of all tornadoes
- EF2–EF3: Strong (winds 111–165 mph) — ~18% of all tornadoes
- EF4–EF5: Violent (winds 166+ mph) — ~2% of all tornadoes, but responsible for
  ~70% of all tornado fatalities

HIGH-RISK COUNTIES — TORNADO EAL ANALYSIS

Moore, Oklahoma (Cleveland County, FIPS: 40027):
Moore is arguably the single most tornado-vulnerable incorporated area in the United States.
It lies directly in a favored tornado track that has produced multiple violent tornadoes.

Historical events:
- May 3, 1999: F5 tornado (Bridge Creek–Moore), winds exceeding 300 mph (highest ever
  measured), 36 deaths, $1 billion damage, 8,000 structures destroyed
- May 20, 2013: EF5 tornado, 24 deaths including 7 children at Plaza Towers Elementary,
  $2 billion damage, 1,200 homes destroyed, 2-mile-wide damage path
- May 31, 2013: EF5 tornado, El Reno–suburban Moore corridor, largest tornado width ever
  recorded at 2.6 miles; 8 deaths including veteran storm chasers

NRI Tornado EAL for Cleveland County, OK: $48 million/year
Tornado events 2000–2023: 47 significant events (EF1+)

Joplin, Missouri (Jasper County, FIPS: 29097):
- May 22, 2011: EF5 tornado struck directly across the city, 161 deaths (deadliest single
  U.S. tornado since 1947), $2.8 billion damage, 7,500 structures destroyed in a 6-mile
  damage path 0.75 miles wide at its peak

NRI Tornado EAL for Jasper County, MO: $32 million/year

Oklahoma County, Oklahoma (FIPS: 40109):
Home to Oklahoma City, one of the largest urban areas in Tornado Alley.
- NRI Tornado EAL: $175 million/year (highest absolute dollar value in Oklahoma)
- EF2+ tornadoes within county 2000–2023: 23 events

WHY CENTRAL PLAINS COUNTIES ARE MORE VULNERABLE

1. Atmospheric dynamics: The dryline — a boundary between Gulf moisture and continental
   dryness — sets up along the western edge of the Plains daily in spring, serving as a
   focal point for supercell thunderstorm development

2. Limited natural barriers: The flat terrain of the central Plains allows supercell
   thunderstorms to travel hundreds of miles without terrain disruption, enabling tornadoes
   to maintain intensity over populated areas

3. Building stock: A high proportion of mobile homes and manufactured housing in rural
   Plains counties provides minimal tornado protection. Mobile homes have a 10× higher
   fatality rate per tornado event than site-built homes

4. Warning time limitations: Despite improvements in Doppler radar (NEXRAD network), the
   average lead time for a tornado warning is 13 minutes — insufficient for evacuation
   of large areas

5. Population density vs. tornado frequency interaction: While rural counties have lower
   absolute EAL, they have higher EAL per capita relative to their total exposure

TORNADO RISK TRENDS 2000–2023

Tornado frequency by decade (NOAA SPC):
- 2000–2009: Average 1,285 tornadoes/year
- 2010–2019: Average 1,141 tornadoes/year (apparent decrease partly due to improved
  verification methods reducing marginal EF0 counts)
- 2020–2023: Average 1,312 tornadoes/year

Notable outbreaks 2010–2023:
- April 25–28, 2011 Super Outbreak: 362 tornadoes in 3 days, 324 deaths, $11 billion
  damage — largest outbreak ever recorded; Alabama, Mississippi, Tennessee most affected
- May 2011: 177 fatalities in one month — deadliest month since 1953
- April 27, 2014 outbreak: 35 tornadoes in Arkansas, Kansas, Nebraska, 17 deaths

Dixie Alley emergence: Research shows the highest per-capita tornado fatality rates in
the 21st century have shifted to the Southeast. Contributing factors include:
- Higher forest cover reduces radar beam penetration and limits visibility
- Higher population density in tornado paths
- Weaker building codes than Midwest states
- Higher mobile home density in rural Southeast counties
- Nocturnal tornado peak (tornados harder to see and respond to at night)

SHELTER AND PREPAREDNESS RECOMMENDATIONS

NOAA and FEMA recommend the following for tornado-prone counties:

1. Community safe rooms: FEMA-rated above-ground or underground shelters rated for
   EF5 winds (250 mph); FEMA provides Hazard Mitigation Grant Program funding

2. Mobile home replacement: Mobile/manufactured homes should never be used as tornado
   shelters; residents should identify permanent shelter locations before severe weather

3. Wireless Emergency Alerts (WEA): Ensure cell phones are tornado warning-enabled;
   outdoor warning sirens supplement but do not replace WEA

4. Storm spotter networks: NOAA's SKYWARN program trains volunteer storm spotters who
   provide ground truth to local NWS offices, improving warning accuracy
