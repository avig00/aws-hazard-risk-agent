FEMA National Risk Index (NRI) — Methodology and Expected Annual Loss

OVERVIEW

The National Risk Index (NRI) is a dataset and online tool developed by FEMA that identifies
communities most at risk for 18 natural hazards. The NRI provides risk, expected annual loss,
social vulnerability, and community resilience scores at the county and census tract level for
the contiguous United States.

EXPECTED ANNUAL LOSS (EAL)

Expected Annual Loss is a core metric in the NRI representing the average economic loss in
dollars resulting from natural hazards each year. EAL is calculated separately for buildings,
agriculture, and population, then combined into a composite score.

The formula for Expected Annual Loss is:

    EAL = Sum over all hazards of (Exposure × Annualized Frequency × Historic Loss Ratio)

Where:
- Exposure: The total value of buildings, agricultural output, or population at risk
- Annualized Frequency: The expected number of hazard events per year based on historical records
- Historic Loss Ratio: The fraction of exposure typically damaged per event, derived from FEMA's
  National Flood Insurance Program, USDA crop loss data, and NOAA storm damage reports

EAL COMPONENTS BY CATEGORY

Building Exposure: Uses U.S. Census Bureau residential building counts multiplied by replacement
costs from RSMeans construction cost data. Commercial and industrial buildings use Dun & Bradstreet
employment data as a proxy.

Agricultural Exposure: Derived from USDA National Agricultural Statistics Service (NASS) cropland
data, including commodity values and irrigation infrastructure.

Population Exposure: Combines U.S. Census population counts with FEMA's value of statistical life
(VSL) methodology — typically $7.6 million per life for risk calculations.

HAZARDS COVERED IN THE NRI

The 18 natural hazards assessed in the NRI are:
1. Avalanche
2. Coastal Flooding
3. Cold Wave
4. Drought
5. Earthquake
6. Hail
7. Heat Wave
8. Hurricane
9. Ice Storm
10. Landslide
11. Lightning
12. Riverine Flooding
13. Strong Wind
14. Tornado
15. Tsunami
16. Volcanic Activity
17. Wildfire
18. Winter Weather

RISK SCORE CALCULATION

The NRI Risk Score combines three composite indices:
- Expected Annual Loss (hazard + exposure)
- Social Vulnerability (from CDC/ATSDR Social Vulnerability Index)
- Community Resilience (from U.S. Census Bureau Community Resilience Estimates)

Risk Score = EAL × Social Vulnerability Factor × (1 / Community Resilience Factor)

Higher social vulnerability amplifies risk because disadvantaged communities have fewer resources
to prepare, respond, and recover. Higher community resilience reduces effective risk.

PERCENTILE RANKINGS

Counties are ranked nationally on a 0–100 percentile scale for each metric. A county at the 90th
percentile for risk has higher risk than 90% of U.S. counties. FEMA uses five risk tiers:
- Very High: 90th–100th percentile
- High: 75th–90th percentile
- Relatively High: 50th–75th percentile
- Relatively Low: 25th–50th percentile
- Very Low: 0–25th percentile

DATA SOURCES

- FEMA National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) claims data
- NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information storm events database
- USGS earthquake catalog and ShakeMap
- USDA cropland data layers and crop insurance records
- U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey
- CDC Social Vulnerability Index (SVI)
- U.S. Census Community Resilience Estimates

LIMITATIONS

The NRI represents average annual conditions and does not predict specific future events. It does
not account for climate change projections, future land use changes, or dynamic population shifts.
EAL estimates are based on historical loss records which may underrepresent rare, high-severity
events. Counties with few historical events may have underestimated risk scores.
