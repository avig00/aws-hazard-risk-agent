FEMA Flood Risk Assessment — Coastal County Vulnerability Report

INTRODUCTION

Coastal counties in the United States face disproportionately high flood risk from two primary
sources: riverine flooding from inland precipitation and coastal flooding from storm surge,
tidal inundation, and sea-level rise. This document summarizes FEMA's assessment of flood
vulnerability for major coastal counties, drawing on National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)
data and the National Risk Index.

HARRIS COUNTY, TEXAS

Harris County (FIPS: 48201) consistently ranks among the highest-risk flood counties in the
United States. The county encompasses Houston, the fourth-largest U.S. city, built largely on
low-lying bayou systems with limited natural drainage.

Key statistics:
- NRI Flood Expected Annual Loss: $1.4 billion (buildings + agriculture + population)
- NFIP policies in force: approximately 500,000 (largest single county in the U.S.)
- Major flood events: Hurricane Harvey (2017) caused $125 billion in total losses,
  flooding over 154,000 structures
- Repetitive loss properties: Harris County has more than 8,000 NFIP repetitive loss
  properties — structures that have filed two or more NFIP claims exceeding 25% of
  the property value in a 10-year period

Flood vulnerability factors for Harris County:
1. Low elevation: Much of the county is within 10–30 feet of sea level
2. Impervious surface cover: Over 60% impervious surfaces reduce infiltration
3. Bayou hydrology: Addicks, Barker, and Brays Bayou all funnel runoff to central Houston
4. Hurricane exposure: Direct Gulf of Mexico access via Galveston Bay
5. Population density: 4.7 million residents, many in flood-prone areas

MIAMI-DADE COUNTY, FLORIDA

Miami-Dade County (FIPS: 12086) faces simultaneous risks from Atlantic hurricanes, coastal
storm surge, and nuisance flooding from sea-level rise. Its flat limestone geology makes
traditional flood barriers ineffective.

Key statistics:
- NRI Hurricane Expected Annual Loss: $4.2 billion
- NRI Coastal Flooding Expected Annual Loss: $890 million
- Elevation: Average elevation under 10 feet above sea level
- Hurricane landfalls since 1900: 11 major hurricanes (Category 3+) within 50 miles

Why Miami-Dade is uniquely vulnerable:
1. Porous limestone aquifer: Seawater intrudes through the limestone bedrock, making
   traditional flood control structures less effective — water rises from below
2. Sea-level rise: Miami has experienced roughly 4 inches of sea-level rise since 1993,
   with king tides increasingly flooding downtown streets during clear weather
3. Dense coastal development: $3.2 trillion in coastal property at risk (Lincoln Institute)
4. Hurricane track probability: Miami sits in the highest hurricane probability zone in
   the continental United States (Atlantic basin)

Notable flood events:
- Hurricane Irma (2017): $50 billion Florida-wide, major surge flooding in Miami Beach
- 2021 Surfside collapse attributed partially to groundwater corrosion

ORLEANS PARISH, LOUISIANA

Orleans Parish (FIPS: 22071) — New Orleans — presents the most extreme case of urban flood
vulnerability in the United States. Much of the city sits 6–8 feet below sea level, entirely
dependent on a system of levees, pumps, and floodwalls.

Key statistics:
- NRI Flood Risk Percentile: 99th (highest nationally)
- NRI Hurricane Expected Annual Loss: $2.8 billion
- Below sea-level area: approximately 50% of the city
- Pump capacity: 24 drainage pumping stations can remove 1 inch of rain per hour
  (insufficient for major tropical events)

Historical losses:
- Hurricane Katrina (2005): $165 billion total loss, 1,800 deaths, 80% of city flooded
  for weeks after levee failures; largest single natural disaster in U.S. history
- Hurricane Ida (2021): $75 billion Louisiana-wide, levees held but extended power outages
  caused significant indirect losses

Systemic vulnerability factors:
1. Subsidence: The city sinks 1–2 inches per year due to groundwater withdrawal and
   natural compaction, increasing flood exposure over time
2. Levee system reliability: Post-Katrina rebuilt $14.5B system; however, Katrina-level
   events remain within the design envelope
3. Environmental buffer loss: Louisiana loses approximately 25–35 square miles of coastal
   wetland annually, removing natural storm surge attenuation

COASTAL VULNERABILITY PATTERNS

Analysis of NFIP data and NRI scores across 387 coastal counties reveals consistent
vulnerability factors:

1. Elevation under 10 feet: Counties with median elevations below 10 feet have 7× higher
   EAL per capita than counties above 50 feet

2. Unconfined coastal aquifers: Permeable geology accelerates saltwater intrusion and
   reduces the effectiveness of traditional flood barriers

3. Hurricane climatology: Counties in the Gulf of Mexico and Atlantic coast from Texas
   to Maine face systematically higher hurricane EAL than Pacific coast counties

4. Land use intensity: Higher impervious surface fraction correlates strongly (r=0.73)
   with flood damage ratios in urban counties

5. NFIP participation: Counties with high NFIP take-up rates tend to have better pre-
   disaster mitigation funding, reducing loss ratios over time

FEMA FLOOD ZONE DESIGNATIONS

FEMA's Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) classify areas by flood probability:
- Zone A / AE: 1% annual chance flood (100-year floodplain) — mandatory NFIP purchase
  for federally backed mortgages
- Zone AO / AH: Sheet flow flooding or ponding with 1% annual chance
- Zone V / VE: Coastal high velocity zones with wave action hazard
- Zone X (shaded): 0.2% annual chance flood (500-year floodplain)
- Zone X (unshaded): Minimal flood hazard

Approximately 8.7 million housing units nationwide are in the 100-year floodplain (Zone A/AE/V).
FEMA estimates that only about 50% of homes in mandatory purchase zones actually hold active
NFIP policies, creating a significant protection gap.

RECOMMENDATIONS FOR HIGH-RISK COUNTIES

1. Mandatory elevation certificates for all new construction in Zones A/V
2. Community Rating System (CRS) participation to reduce NFIP premiums through mitigation
3. Acquisition programs for repetitive loss properties in the most flood-prone areas
4. Coastal wetland and mangrove restoration to rebuild natural storm surge buffers
5. Updated flood maps incorporating LiDAR elevation data and projected sea-level rise
