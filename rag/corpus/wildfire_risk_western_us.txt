USDA Forest Service / FEMA — Wildfire Risk Assessment for Western United States Counties

OVERVIEW

Wildfire is one of the fastest-growing natural hazard threats in the United States. The
NRI identifies wildfire as the primary hazard for hundreds of western counties. The
Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) — the zone where human development meets or intermingles
with unmanaged wildland vegetation — now encompasses approximately 46 million homes and
30% of all U.S. housing units.

Between 2000 and 2023, the U.S. experienced over 100,000 wildfires per year on average,
burning 6–10 million acres annually. The economic cost has escalated dramatically, from
roughly $1–2 billion annually in the 2000s to $10–20 billion in the 2020s.

CRITICAL WILDFIRE COUNTIES

Los Angeles County, California (FIPS: 06037):
Los Angeles County represents the largest wildfire exposure in the country in absolute
dollar terms, combining a massive building stock with fire-prone chaparral terrain.

Key vulnerability factors:
1. Santa Ana winds: Seasonal offshore winds (October–December) can exceed 80 mph,
   driving fires rapidly through dry chaparral at speeds of 1–2 miles per hour
2. Mediterranean climate: Hot, dry summers cure vegetation to low moisture levels
   (below 10%), creating explosive fire conditions
3. WUI density: The Santa Monica Mountains, San Gabriel Mountains, and Santa Ana Mountains
   all border heavily populated valleys with limited evacuation routes

Notable events:
- 2018 Woolsey Fire: 96,949 acres, 3 deaths, 1,643 structures destroyed, $6 billion loss;
  forced evacuation of 295,000 residents including Malibu and Calabasas
- 2020 Bobcat Fire: 115,796 acres in the Angeles National Forest, $1.4 billion damage
- 2025 Palisades and Eaton Fires (January 2025): Combined destruction of over 12,000
  structures in Pacific Palisades and Altadena; estimated $50+ billion economic loss;
  deadliest California fire in a century

NRI Wildfire EAL for Los Angeles County: $2.4 billion/year
NRI Wildfire Risk Percentile: 97th nationally

Mariposa County, California (FIPS: 06043):
Home to Yosemite Valley, Mariposa County is the epicenter of Sierra Nevada wildfire risk.

- 2018 Ferguson Fire: Forced Yosemite Valley closure, $8.8 million suppression cost
- NRI Wildfire EAL per capita: Among the highest in California

Larimer County, Colorado (FIPS: 08069):
The Colorado Front Range faces increasing wildfire pressure from drought and beetle kill.

- 2021 Cameron Peak Fire: 208,913 acres — largest fire in Colorado history; $200 million
- 2021 East Troublesome Fire: 193,812 acres, 2 deaths, burned into Rocky Mountain National Park
- 2021 Marshall Fire (December 2021): 6,026 acres in Boulder County suburb, 1,084 homes
  destroyed — largest structure loss in Colorado history; driven by 100 mph wind gusts
  over drought-cured grass, demonstrating that WUI fires can occur even in cold months

WILDFIRE RISK FACTORS BY COUNTY TYPE

Chaparral/Shrubland Counties (CA, AZ, NM):
- Fire return interval historically 30–100 years
- Post-suppression era fuel accumulation has compressed effective fire cycles
- Fast-moving surface fires with spotting ahead of the fire front

Forested Mountain Counties (CA Sierra, CO Rockies, OR/WA Cascades):
- Bark beetle-killed trees (blue stain fungus from Dendroctonus ponderosae) create
  massive standing dead fuel loads — over 130 million dead trees in California by 2019
- Crown fires in dense forest can release 10,000+ BTU/ft² — suppression is often impossible
- High-severity fires leave watersheds vulnerable to post-fire debris flows

Grassland Counties (Great Plains/Intermountain West):
- Fast-spreading grass fires can travel 10+ miles per day
- Difficult to defend: fire lines are difficult to hold in grass
- Kansas, Oklahoma, and Nebraska have experienced large grass fires that devastated farms

CLIMATE DRIVERS OF INCREASING WILDFIRE RISK

The relationship between climate variables and wildfire activity is well-established:

1. Drought: Palmer Drought Severity Index below -2.0 correlates with 4× higher burn area
   Consecutive dry years compound fuel dryness progressively

2. Temperature: Each 1°C increase in average summer temperature is associated with a
   12% increase in fire season length

3. Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD): High VPD (low atmospheric moisture) is the single
   strongest predictor of daily fire weather; VPD above 25 hPa creates extreme fire
   weather conditions

4. Wind: Fire spread is proportional to wind speed squared; Santa Ana and Diablo wind
   events (California), Chinook/Foehn winds (Colorado) are key extreme fire weather drivers

5. Snow drought: Reduced mountain snowpack means lower summer soil moisture, extending
   the fire season into fall

FEMA WILDFIRE MITIGATION RECOMMENDATIONS

1. Defensible space: Clear 0–30 feet of Zone 1 (ember-resistant) and 30–100 feet of
   Zone 2 (reduced fuel) around all structures in WUI areas

2. Home hardening: Replace wood shake roofs with Class A fire-rated materials; install
   ember-resistant vents; use dual-pane or tempered glass windows; enclose eaves

3. Community Wildfire Protection Plans (CWPPs): Collaborative risk assessments developed
   with fire agencies, local governments, and homeowners identify priority fuel treatment areas

4. Ignition-resistant communities: Utility pole replacement, undergrounding power lines
   in high-risk corridors, public land prescribed burn programs

5. Insurance: FEMA recommends all WUI homeowners carry structure replacement cost coverage
   with extended replacement cost endorsements given post-fire construction cost spikes
